# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Thai children aged 5–10, using the app independently on a smartphone or tablet. The child is the primary actor — they navigate, tap, listen, and answer without a parent present. Sessions are short (5–10 minutes), often in downtime at home or between activities. The child needs to feel immediately capable, not confused; every tap should produce a satisfying, legible result.

Secondary user: a parent who installs the app and occasionally checks progress. They care that the app feels safe, purposeful, and visually clean — not chaotic.

## Product Purpose

Little Learners is a bilingual vocabulary practice app for Thai children learning English (picture + audio flashcards, quizzes) and Mandarin Chinese (HSK 1 words with pinyin, stroke order, and audio). The app uses a two-track structure: an English path and a Chinese path, each with vocabulary organized by category.

Success looks like a child completing a category, earning a star, and wanting to immediately start the next one. The product is habit-forming through streaks and star rewards, not through pressure. A session should end with the child feeling capable and accomplished.

## Brand Personality

Encouraging, clear, celebratory.

Voice is warm and direct — written primarily in Thai for the child, with English and Chinese as the content being learned. Feedback is immediate and positive. The interface never scolds; it celebrates partial wins and invites retries without shame.

Emotional goal: the child should feel like they're playing a game, not doing homework.

## Anti-references

- **Generic "educational software" aesthetics**: clip-art icons, rainbow color-everywhere, low-contrast text on bright backgrounds. Looks cheap and is hard to read outdoors.
- **Overly adult app conventions**: thin typography, subtle animations, monochromatic minimalism. Kids need clear affordances and visual feedback.
- **Loud cartoon chaos**: too many competing visual elements at once — animations firing everywhere, every card a different bright color, no visual rest. Exhausting, not delightful.
- **Heavy gamification clutter**: achievement popups stacking on each other, notifications, leaderboards. This is a solo practice app, not a competitive game. Progress is personal.
- **Baby-app aesthetics**: big round cartoon characters, pastel blobs, smiley faces on every button. The 5–10 age group doesn't want to feel like they're using a toddler toy.

## Design Principles

1. **The child is capable.** Every screen should assume the child can navigate it without help. Affordances must be large, legible, and unambiguous — but not infantilizing.
2. **One thing at a time.** Each screen has one primary action. The category grid is for choosing. The flashcard is for studying. The quiz is for testing. Never merge these jobs or crowd the screen.
3. **Reward progress, not perfection.** Stars, streaks, and completion badges should appear as natural consequences of effort, not as pressure to perform. A wrong quiz answer should feel like a helpful hint, not a penalty.
4. **Premium means intentional.** Elevated kids design isn't about removing color or fun — it's about using both with purpose. Color identifies, not decorates. Animation responds, not performs.
5. **Legibility first.** The child reads Thai, English, and Chinese simultaneously. Typography must be clear and large enough for emerging readers on a phone screen in variable lighting.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Touch targets minimum 44×44px (critical for 5–10 year old motor control). High contrast between text and background — the child may use the app outdoors in bright sunlight. Audio is a core learning mechanic; all vocabulary has audio playback. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` — celebration animations should not be required for comprehension. Thai is the UI language; English and Chinese are content languages.
